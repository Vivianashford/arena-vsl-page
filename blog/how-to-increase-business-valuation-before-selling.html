How to Increase Business Valuation Before Selling: The Moves That Actually Move the Multiple

JH
Jean Louis Hardy The Arena Partners
February 15, 2026 • 10 min read

Most business owners, when they start thinking seriously about selling, ask the same question: "How do I get the highest price?" The answer is not what they expect. It's not about growing revenue faster in the last year. It's not about buying new equipment to impress a buyer. And it's definitely not about timing the market.

The businesses that sell at 5.0x to 6.5x EBITDA instead of 3.0x to 3.5x got there through operational discipline that started 18 to 36 months before the sale. The gap between those multiples - on a $1.2M EBITDA business - is $3.6M to $3.96M in deal value. That's the magnitude of what we're talking about.

Here is where that value actually comes from.

Move 1: Maximize and Document Adjusted EBITDA

Buyers pay on adjusted EBITDA. Not revenue. Not net income. Not what you think the business earns. They pay on a specifically calculated, defended, and agreed-upon EBITDA figure that accounts for legitimate add-backs to normalize the business economics.

If you're not actively tracking and documenting your add-backs, you're leaving money on the table - not because your business is worth less, but because you can't prove it's worth more.

Common add-backs in trade businesses that owners often under-document:

  • Owner compensation above market replacement rate. If a GM to replace you would cost $95,000 and you're paying yourself $280,000, the $185,000 difference is an add-back to normalized EBITDA.
  • Family members on payroll at above-market rates. If your spouse is a bookkeeper and earns $85,000 but market rate is $55,000, $30,000 is an add-back.
  • Personal vehicles, phone plans, health insurance, and other personal costs run through the business.
  • One-time legal costs, equipment write-offs, or significant non-recurring expenses.
  • Depreciation on assets that have already been replaced and won't need replacement again for years.

Work with an accountant or M&A advisor to build a full add-back schedule with documentation for each item. This schedule becomes part of your offering materials. A well-supported add-back schedule that increases normalized EBITDA from $1.1M to $1.4M, at a 5x multiple, is a $1.5M difference in deal value.

Move 2: Build Recurring Revenue

If there's a single operational move that increases business valuation most reliably for trade businesses, it's adding structured, recurring service revenue. Not because it sounds good - because buyers model it differently.

Recurring revenue - maintenance agreements, service contracts, annual inspection programs - is more predictable than project revenue. Buyers apply lower risk assumptions to it. That lower risk assumption translates directly into a higher multiple.

A plumbing business with 15% recurring revenue and a project-heavy base might sell at 3.5x. A plumbing business with 40% recurring revenue and strong service agreement retention might sell at 5.0x or higher. Same size, same owner, different structure - and a completely different transaction outcome.

Building a service agreement program doesn't happen in 90 days. It requires a sales process, technician training, software to manage recurring scheduling, and enough time for the program to prove its retention rates. Plan 18 to 24 months for a program that has enough track record to meaningfully affect your valuation conversation.

Move 3: Install Operational Leadership

As covered in detail in our piece on owner-dependent business valuation, the single biggest multiple discount in trade businesses comes from owner dependency. Fixing it requires installing someone who can run the operation without you.

A general manager or operations director who has been running the business for 12+ months before you go to market is one of the most concrete signals you can send to a buyer. It proves the business can function post-close. It reduces transition risk. It enables a cleaner deal structure - less earnout, more cash at close.

The ROI on this hire is unusually high. A GM at $120,000 per year for two years costs $240,000. If that hire expands your multiple from 3.5x to 5.0x on a $1.3M EBITDA business, the value created is $1.95M. That's an 8-to-1 return on a specific, planned investment.

Move 4: Clean Up the Financial Presentation

Buyers and their advisors spend significant time in due diligence trying to understand what your financials actually say. If your books are disorganized, inconsistently categorized, or require extensive explanation to interpret, that creates friction - and friction creates doubt.

Three specific financial cleanup actions that matter:

Consistent P&L categorization for three years. If you reclassified expenses between years, changed how you handle owner draws, or use different category structures year to year, buyers can't easily build a normalized view of the business. Clean, consistent, comparable financials are the foundation of a smooth diligence process.

Separation of business and personal expenses. Mixed financials - personal credit cards on the business, household expenses running through the company account - are not automatically disqualifying, but they require detailed explanation in every buyer conversation. Cleaning them up before going to market removes the distraction.

Solid accounts receivable management. Aging receivables are a diligence concern. A clean AR aging report - with minimal accounts over 90 days and no large uncollectible balances - presents a much healthier picture of the business's cash conversion cycle.

A multiple expansion from 3.5x to 5.0x on a $1.3M EBITDA business is $1.95 million in additional proceeds. That's the scale of what's at stake.

Move 5: Reduce Customer Concentration

Customer concentration is a valuation discount that many trade business owners don't take seriously enough. If one client represents 25% or more of your revenue, a sophisticated buyer will immediately model what happens if that client doesn't transfer post-acquisition - or renegotiates terms after you leave.

The mitigation strategy depends on your situation. Options include:

  • Actively growing other accounts to reduce the concentration percentage over time
  • Securing a longer-term contract with the concentrated client before going to market
  • Getting the key contact at that client to meet your management team and document the relationship's transferability

There's no quick fix. But a business that enters a sale process with no single client above 15% of revenue is a materially lower-risk proposition than one where a single account could walk.

Move 6: Resolve Legal and Compliance Issues

Unresolved legal disputes, licensing compliance issues, or OSHA violations that haven't been addressed are deal killers or price reducers in due diligence. Buyers don't want to inherit unknown liabilities. When they find them, they either walk or ask for a significant price reduction plus escrow holdback.

Before going to market, do a clean sweep of:

  • Active or threatened litigation
  • Licensing status for all jurisdictions you operate in
  • Workers' comp claims history and open matters
  • Environmental compliance for any commercial or industrial operations
  • Subcontractor classification (employee vs. 1099 status is under heavy scrutiny in many states)

What Doesn't Move the Multiple (As Much as You Think)

A few common misconceptions about how to increase business valuation before selling:

Buying new equipment. Capital investment in trucks and tools looks good to you. Buyers model it as capex that reduces free cash flow. They're not paying a premium for new trucks - they're adjusting their model to reflect the expected replacement cycle of your existing fleet. New equipment bought in the 12 months before a sale rarely adds value dollar-for-dollar.

Rushing revenue growth in the final year. A single year of strong revenue growth, without the prior years to support it, often raises questions rather than confidence. Buyers look at multi-year trends. A business that grew 40% in year three but was flat in years one and two triggers questions about sustainability. Consistent growth over three years is far more compelling than a spike.

Making cosmetic changes to the business. New logos, refreshed uniforms, and website updates are fine - but they don't move multiples. What moves multiples is operational infrastructure that a buyer can verify, analyze, and underwrite. Focus your preparation energy there.

The Timeline That Makes This Work

Understanding how to increase business valuation before selling is straightforward. Executing it requires time. Most of the moves above - building recurring revenue, installing leadership, cleaning up financials, addressing concentration - take 18 to 24 months to show up meaningfully in the business.

If you want to go to market in two years, start today. If you're planning to exit in five years, you have time to do this properly and compound the results.
