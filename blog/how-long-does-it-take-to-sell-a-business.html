How Long Does It Take to Sell a Business? A Realistic Timeline for Trade Business Owners

JH
Jean Louis Hardy The Arena Partners
January 18, 2026 • 7 min read

The first question most business owners ask when they start thinking seriously about selling is: how long does it take to sell a business? The honest answer is that it depends entirely on what you count as the beginning.

If you count from the day you engage an advisor and start the formal sale process, a well-run transaction takes 6 to 12 months to close. If you count from the day you begin the preparation work that actually determines the price you'll receive, the real timeline is 18 to 36 months.

Most owners plan for the first number and are surprised by the second. The result is either a rushed sale at a discounted price, or a longer-than-expected preparation period that tests their patience. The owners who understand both timelines from the start are the ones who exit on their own terms.

The Full Timeline: From Decision to Close

Here's how the complete process breaks down for a typical trade business in the $5M to $20M revenue range.

Phase 1: Preparation (12 to 24 months before going to market)

This is the phase most business owners skip - and it's where the most value is either created or left on the table.

Preparation includes:

  • Getting a quality-of-earnings analysis to understand what buyers will find in your financials
  • Building and documenting your adjusted EBITDA with legitimate add-backs
  • Addressing owner dependency by installing or developing a management layer
  • Cleaning up financial reporting to ensure three years of consistent, comparable P&L
  • Building recurring revenue programs (service agreements, maintenance contracts)
  • Resolving any legal, licensing, or compliance issues before buyers discover them
  • Reducing customer concentration where possible

This phase takes as long as it takes. A business that's largely ready might need 12 months of focused preparation. A business with significant owner dependency and inconsistent financials might need 24 to 30 months. The question isn't how to rush it - it's how to invest this time in the changes that will move the multiple most meaningfully.

Phase 2: Advisor Selection and Positioning (2 to 3 months)

Once the business is prepared for market, selecting the right advisor and developing the marketing materials adds 2 to 3 months. A quality advisor will:

  • Develop a detailed Confidential Information Memorandum presenting the business to buyers
  • Prepare a financial model that clearly shows adjusted EBITDA and add-backs
  • Identify and qualify the buyer universe (strategic acquirers, PE platforms, individual buyers)
  • Structure the process to create competitive tension among multiple buyers

Don't rush this phase. The quality of the CIM and the composition of the buyer list have real impact on the offers you receive. A well-prepared business presented to the wrong buyers is still a below-market transaction.

Phase 3: Marketing and Initial Offers (2 to 4 months)

Once the business is in market, the advisor reaches out to qualified buyers under NDA. Buyers review the CIM, ask follow-up questions, and submit Indications of Interest (IOIs) - non-binding initial offers that indicate price range and key terms.

The timeline for this phase depends on:

  • How many qualified buyers exist for your business type and size
  • How quickly buyers respond to outreach
  • Whether the initial offers require additional information before advancing

Two to four months is typical. A business with a well-defined buyer universe and strong positioning can receive IOIs faster. A more specialized business - a niche industrial service company, for example - may take longer to find the right acquirers.

Phase 4: Management Presentations and LOI (1 to 2 months)

After IOIs are received and shortlisted, serious buyers participate in management presentations - formal meetings with the seller and their key team. This is followed by Letter of Intent negotiations.

The LOI is a critical document. It establishes the purchase price, deal structure, key terms, and exclusivity period. Sellers who negotiate the LOI carefully - understanding the downstream implications of earnout structure, seller note terms, and rep and warranty provisions - are in a much better position at close than those who accept the first version presented.

Plan 30 to 60 days from initial management presentations to a signed LOI. Complex deal structures or competing buyers can extend this, but urgency typically benefits both parties at this stage.

Phase 5: Due Diligence (60 to 120 days)

Once an LOI is signed, the exclusivity clock starts and formal due diligence begins. Buyers bring in accountants, attorneys, and operational advisors to verify everything you've represented about the business.

A well-prepared seller moves through diligence in 60 days. A seller who didn't prepare - who is producing documents reactively, who is explaining inconsistencies that weren't disclosed upfront, who is managing surprises as buyers find them - often extends due diligence to 90 or 120 days, sometimes losing the deal entirely.

Where deals most often stall or fall apart during diligence:

  • Financial inconsistencies between represented numbers and underlying records
  • Undisclosed customer losses or revenue declines in recent months
  • Key employee departures or indications of team instability
  • Legal matters, liens, or licensing issues that weren't disclosed
  • Owner dependency that's more severe than initially presented

For more detail on what buyers find and how to prepare for it, read our article on what buyers look for when buying a business.

A prepared seller moves through due diligence in 60 days. An unprepared seller watches their deal renegotiate or fall apart over four months.

Phase 6: Purchase Agreement and Close (30 to 60 days)

After due diligence is complete, lawyers on both sides negotiate and finalize the definitive purchase agreement. This is the binding legal document that governs the sale. It includes representations and warranties, indemnification provisions, post-close obligations, and all the deal terms that flow from the LOI.

For straightforward deals with aligned parties and experienced counsel, this phase takes 30 days. For complex transactions with multiple entities, significant earnout provisions, or disagreements over rep and warranty scope, 60 days or more is common.

The Total Timeline at a Glance

Adding these phases together for a typical trade business sale:

  • Preparation: 12 to 24 months
  • Advisor selection and positioning: 2 to 3 months
  • Marketing and initial offers: 2 to 4 months
  • Management presentations and LOI: 1 to 2 months
  • Due diligence: 2 to 4 months
  • Purchase agreement and close: 1 to 2 months

Total from decision to close: 20 to 39 months.

The formal sale process - from advisor engagement through close - runs 8 to 15 months for a well-prepared business. The business that isn't prepared takes longer through every phase and often transacts at a lower price to compensate buyers for the additional risk they're absorbing.

What Causes Deals to Take Longer (or Fall Apart)

Beyond the preparation issues already mentioned, there are structural factors that extend timelines:

Financing-dependent buyers. Individual buyers using SBA loans add 45 to 60 days to a timeline because bank approval takes time. PE-backed buyers and strategic acquirers who can use their own capital or established credit lines move faster.

Complex entity structures. Multiple LLCs, mixed personal and business assets, real estate entangled in the operating company - these structures take longer to untangle in legal diligence and purchase agreement drafting.

Seller indecision. Founders who are emotionally uncertain about whether they actually want to sell can stretch or kill a process. Buyers sense ambivalence and sometimes walk away from uncertain sellers - especially if another transaction opportunity is available.

Market timing surprises. Interest rate changes, economic disruptions, or industry-specific events can slow a process that was moving cleanly. There's no fully eliminating market timing risk, but a business that's genuinely strong weathers these disruptions better than one that's marginally positioned.

The Right Way to Think About the Timeline

The question "how long does it take to sell a business" is better framed as "how long until my business is worth what I want it to be worth, and I'm ready to sell it?"

For most trade business owners reading this, the answer to that question - if it's not already "I'm ready" - is 18 to 30 months of focused preparation. That's not a discouraging answer. It's a clarifying one. It means the work starts now, the preparation phase is productive and purposeful, and the formal sale process becomes the culmination of something built deliberately - not a desperate sprint.
