What Happens in Due Diligence: The 60-Day Gauntlet Every Seller Faces

JH
Jean Louis Hardy The Arena Partners
May 2, 2026 • 10 min read

You have signed a letter of intent. The buyer has agreed to your price. Your advisor is telling you the deal is as good as done. And then due diligence begins — and everything you thought was settled gets picked apart, questioned, and in some cases, used against you.

Due diligence is where deals die. Studies of middle-market M&A transactions consistently show that 25% to 35% of deals under a signed LOI fail to close. The overwhelming majority of those failures happen during the due diligence phase. Not because the business was bad. Because the seller was not prepared for what was coming.

This is the most important 60 days of your financial life. Here is exactly what happens — and what you need to know before you get there.

25–35% of deals with a signed LOI never close. Most fall apart during due diligence — not because the business failed, but because the seller wasn't ready for the scrutiny.

What Due Diligence Actually Is

Due diligence is the buyer's right to verify everything you told them before they signed the LOI. Every number, every claim, every representation. They are not taking your word for it. They are hiring accountants, lawyers, operations consultants, and sometimes environmental engineers to confirm that the business they agreed to buy is the business you actually have.

For a service business doing $5M to $30M in revenue, you should expect the buyer to request hundreds of documents across multiple workstreams. They will likely run these in parallel — financial due diligence and legal due diligence happening at the same time, with operational and HR reviews layered on top. For a PE buyer, this is a routine process they have done dozens of times. For most sellers, it is entirely new territory.

The information asymmetry is real. The buyer has done this before. You probably have not. That gap is exploitable — and sophisticated buyers know it.

The Four Workstreams Buyers Run Simultaneously

Financial Due Diligence (QofE)

The buyer's accountants will conduct a Quality of Earnings analysis — a deep-dive into your financial statements that goes well beyond anything your annual audit or tax return covers. They are looking at three to five years of financials and asking hard questions about every line item.

They want to know: Is the EBITDA you reported real and recurring? Are there add-backs that are legitimate? Are there costs you excluded that should be included? Is revenue recognized correctly? Are there related-party transactions that distort the picture? Is working capital consistent or does it swing wildly?

A QofE typically takes four to six weeks and costs the buyer $50,000 to $150,000 for a business in your size range. They are paying that much because the findings directly determine whether the price holds, gets reduced, or whether they walk away entirely.

The most common QofE findings that kill deals or reduce prices:

  • EBITDA that turns out to be $200K to $500K lower than represented after removing unsupported add-backs
  • Revenue that is not as recurring as presented — one-time projects billed as recurring contracts
  • Unusual working capital fluctuations that indicate the business cannot sustain its reported margins
  • Undisclosed related-party transactions — payments to family members, below-market rent from owner-owned real estate, personal expenses run through the business
The average QofE finds $300K–$600K in EBITDA adjustments on a $5M–$15M service business. At a 6x multiple, that is $1.8M–$3.6M off your price — from a single workstream.

Legal Due Diligence

The buyer's lawyers will review every contract, every liability, and every legal exposure in the business. This includes customer contracts, supplier agreements, equipment leases, real estate leases, employee agreements, non-competes, pending or past litigation, regulatory compliance, licenses, permits, and insurance policies.

What kills deals in legal DD:

  • Customer contracts that are not assignable — meaning the buyer cannot take them over without customer consent, which introduces deal risk
  • Undisclosed litigation or regulatory issues that represent contingent liabilities
  • Licenses or permits that do not transfer with the business
  • Environmental liability, particularly for companies that handle chemicals, waste, or operate on industrial sites
  • Personal guarantees or debt that is attached to the business and not clearly resolvable at closing

Operational Due Diligence

For PE buyers in particular, operational DD is as important as financial DD. They want to understand how the business actually runs. They will interview your management team, walk your facilities, review your technology systems, and assess how dependent the business is on you as the owner.

Owner dependency is the single most common operational finding that suppresses value. If the buyer determines that you are the relationship with every major customer, the technical lead on every major project, and the decision-maker on every significant issue, they have acquired a problem — not a business. They will price it that way.

HR and Organizational Due Diligence

Buyers will review your org chart, employee compensation and benefits, employment agreements, non-competes, and key employee retention risk. For service businesses where people are the product, this workstream carries significant weight.

They are looking for: key employees who could leave post-close; compensation structures that are not competitive; labor law compliance issues; and union exposure or pending employment disputes.

The Retrade: When Buyers Use Due Diligence to Renegotiate

A retrade is when a buyer uses findings from due diligence to renegotiate the price after the LOI is signed. It is the thing sellers fear most, and it happens more often than the industry likes to admit.

Some retrades are legitimate. If due diligence reveals that EBITDA was overstated by $400K, a price reduction is reasonable. The number you agreed to was based on information that turned out to be wrong.

But some retrades are tactics. A sophisticated buyer — particularly a PE firm that has done hundreds of acquisitions — knows that after 60 days of due diligence, you are emotionally and financially committed to the deal. Your employees may already know something is happening. You may have already started planning your post-close life. They use that commitment against you, presenting findings that justify a price reduction when the real justification is that they have leverage and they know it.

The best defense against tactical retrades is preparation. Sellers who have run a pre-sale due diligence process — who know exactly what a buyer will find before the buyer finds it — are far more resistant to price games. When a buyer says "we found this issue," the prepared seller says "yes, we disclosed that in our data room on day one, and our price reflects it." That conversation goes very differently than when the seller is hearing the issue for the first time.

Sellers who prepare a virtual data room before going to market reduce due diligence timelines by an average of 40% and experience significantly fewer retrade attempts. Preparation is negotiating leverage.

The Data Room: What You Need Before You Start

A data room is a secure, organized digital repository of every document a buyer will request. Building it before you go to market — not scrambling to assemble it after you have a signed LOI — is one of the most important things you can do to protect your price and your timeline.

The core contents of a data room for a service business:

  • Financial: Three to five years of tax returns and financial statements, monthly P&Ls for the trailing 24 months, accounts receivable and payable aging, backlog and pipeline reports, list of all add-backs with documentation
  • Legal: All customer contracts, supplier agreements, equipment leases, real estate leases, licenses and permits, corporate formation documents, minutes from any board or shareholder meetings
  • Operational: Organizational chart, employee census with compensation, equipment list with condition and age, technology systems overview, documented processes and SOPs
  • Customer: Customer concentration analysis (top 10 customers as a percentage of revenue), contract terms and renewal history, any customer at risk of loss
  • HR: Employment agreements for key employees, non-compete and non-solicitation agreements, benefits summary, any pending or past employment disputes

Sellers who hand buyers a complete, organized data room on day one of due diligence signal something important: this owner knows their business, has nothing to hide, and has managed this company like a professional. That signal matters. It changes how the buyer treats the entire process.

What Kills Deals in Due Diligence

After advising on dozens of service business sales, the same issues surface again and again as deal killers. Some are fixable with preparation. Others take years to address. Here are the most common:

Customer concentration. A single customer representing more than 20% to 25% of revenue is a red flag for almost every buyer. It introduces existential risk — if that customer leaves post-close, the business the buyer paid for no longer exists. PE buyers will either walk away, reduce the price significantly, or structure a portion of the payment as an earnout tied to customer retention.

Undisclosed liabilities. Environmental issues, pending litigation, regulatory violations, and tax liabilities that were not disclosed upfront do not just reduce price — they often kill deals entirely. Buyers interpret non-disclosure as evidence of broader integrity issues in the seller's representations.

Revenue quality problems. If the QofE finds that a material portion of reported revenue was from one-time sources — a large non-recurring contract, a related-party transaction, a government grant — the adjusted EBITDA may be significantly lower than what was represented. At a 6x to 8x multiple, a $300K EBITDA overstatement is a $1.8M to $2.4M price problem.

Key person risk. If the business cannot demonstrate that it will continue to operate effectively after the owner exits, buyers cannot underwrite the acquisition at a premium price. This is often the hardest issue to fix during a deal — you cannot manufacture a management team in 60 days. It has to be built over years.

Messy books. Financial statements prepared primarily for tax minimization — with owner perks, personal expenses, and related-party transactions woven throughout — take significant work to normalize. Buyers lose confidence when financials are hard to understand. Clean books are not just an accounting issue. They are a trust issue.

How to Survive Due Diligence with Your Price Intact

There are three things that consistently separate sellers who close at their LOI price from sellers who get retrades or lose their deals entirely.

Prepare before you go to market. Run a pre-sale QofE. Build your data room before you start talking to buyers. Identify every issue a buyer will find before they find it. Disclose proactively. Price issues that are known upfront are priced once. Issues discovered in due diligence are priced at a multiplied cost: the original adjustment plus the credibility damage of having not disclosed it.

Maintain business performance during the process. Due diligence takes 45 to 90 days. During that time, the business has to keep performing. Deals collapse when buyers see mid-process financials that show deteriorating performance — even temporary dips that have reasonable explanations. Stay focused on operations while the deal is running.

Have representation who has been through this before. The information asymmetry between a first-time seller and a team of PE professionals who have done this hundreds of times is enormous. Experienced M&A advisors have seen every buyer tactic, know how to respond to every retrade, and understand which findings are legitimate and which are negotiating games. That experience is worth far more than their fee.

The average seller leaves 15-20% of their deal value on the table due to poor preparation and lack of experienced representation. On a $10M deal, that is $1.5M to $2M — real money that stays with the buyer instead of you.

The Bottom Line

Due diligence is not something that happens to you. It is something you can prepare for, shape, and in many cases, control the outcome of. The sellers who close at their price are not lucky. They are prepared. They built their data room before they needed it. They fixed their issues before a buyer found them. They hired advisors who knew what was coming.

If you are thinking about selling your business in the next one to three years, the work you do today — cleaning up financials, reducing customer concentration, building a management team, documenting your processes — is not just good operations. It is deal preparation. And it pays off in seven figures when the time comes.
