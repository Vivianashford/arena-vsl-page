Selling a Business at 60: What's Different, What Matters More, and How to Get It Right

JH
Jean Louis Hardy The Arena Partners
February 8, 2026 • 9 min read

The average age of a first-time business seller in the trades is 61. They've been running their company for 20 to 30 years. And in almost every case, the conversation about selling started too late - not too early.

Selling a business at 60 is a fundamentally different exercise than selling at 45. Not harder, necessarily - but different in ways that matter enormously. The tax implications change. The priorities around deal structure shift. The emotional calculus is different. And critically, the runway to recover from a bad outcome is much shorter.

If you're 58, 60, or 62 and starting to think seriously about what comes next, this piece is written for you.

Why the Math Is Different at 60

A 45-year-old who sells a business and ends up with less than expected has time to build again, adjust lifestyle, or wait for a second opportunity. A 60-year-old who nets $2.8M instead of $4.5M because they didn't prepare properly has a materially different retirement than the one they planned for. There's no Act Two that fixes it.

This is the first thing to internalize when thinking about selling a business at 60: the cost of mistakes is asymmetric. Preparation time, deal structure, tax planning - errors in each of these categories carry consequences that compound for decades, not years.

The financial planning questions that become urgent at 60:

  • What do you need from the sale proceeds to fund 25 to 30 years of retirement comfortably?
  • What's your current retirement asset base outside the business?
  • How dependent is your retirement plan on the business sale? (Many trade business owners have most of their net worth in the business - this is a concentration risk that the sale is meant to solve)
  • What does Social Security look like for you at 67 vs. 62 vs. 70?
  • Do you have healthcare coverage independent of the business, or does that need to be planned for between sale and Medicare eligibility?

These questions aren't meant to create anxiety. They're meant to clarify the target. Knowing exactly what you need from the sale - not just a vague sense that you want "a lot" - is what allows you to negotiate from clarity rather than desperation.

Tax Planning Becomes Critical

At 60, the tax structure of your sale is not an afterthought. It's one of the highest-ROI planning conversations you can have.

The key variables:

Asset sale vs. stock sale. Most trade business acquisitions are structured as asset sales, which is generally better for buyers (stepped-up basis, no inherited liabilities) and worse for sellers (ordinary income tax on certain asset categories). If you're structured as an S-Corp or C-Corp, the difference between asset and stock sale treatment can be significant - sometimes $200,000 to $500,000 or more in tax liability. This needs to be negotiated in the LOI, not after it's signed.

Installment sale treatment. If you accept a seller note as part of the deal structure, you may be able to spread recognition of the gain over multiple years, keeping you in a lower tax bracket annually. This is a meaningful planning opportunity if you're selling in a high-income year and facing a compressed tax hit otherwise.

Qualified Opportunity Zone reinvestment, Charitable Remainder Trusts, and Qualified Small Business Stock exemptions. These are specialized strategies that apply in specific situations. I'm not a tax advisor - but I'd recommend anyone selling a business above $3M work with a CPA who has done M&A transaction tax planning, not just annual returns. The difference in a single conversation can be substantial.

Entity structure cleanup before the sale. If you've been operating as a sole proprietor or have complex entity structures, cleaning these up before going to market simplifies the transaction and removes potential deal complications. This ideally happens 12 to 24 months before a target close.

Deal Structure Priorities Shift at 60

A 40-year-old seller might enthusiastically accept an earnout structure that pays a significant portion of the deal price over three years tied to revenue growth. They're staying involved, they're energized, and they believe in the business's trajectory.

A 60-year-old seller should be skeptical of that structure. Here's why.

Earnouts require the seller to operate under the strategic decisions of the new owner - with their own capital at risk. If the new owner changes the business model, reduces marketing spend, or integrates operations in ways that affect revenue, the earnout might not pay. And at 60, being contractually tethered to a business you've already mentally sold - for three years, with payment uncertainty - is not a retirement.

When selling a business at 60, the priority order for deal proceeds generally looks like this:

  1. Cash at close - target 75 to 85% of the total deal value in immediate, unconditional cash
  2. Seller note with strong terms - shorter duration (3 to 5 years), secured against business assets, with clear default protections
  3. Equity rollover - only if the acquiring platform is credible and you can afford to wait for a subsequent liquidity event (which could be 5+ years away)
  4. Earnout - accept this only if the earnout metrics are things you control directly, the duration is short (12 to 24 months maximum), and the earnout component is small relative to cash at close

This doesn't mean you can't close a good deal with some deferred components. It means the structure of those components should match your actual situation - not a theoretical seller profile that doesn't account for age and retirement timeline.

At 60, the question isn't just what is the business worth - it's what do I actually walk away with after tax, after structure, after transition costs.

The Emotional Reality No One Talks About

Selling a business at 60 is not just a financial transaction. It's the end of an identity that most founders have held for two or three decades. The business is how you define yourself. It's how your community knows you. It's your schedule, your purpose, and often your largest social circle.

The founders who navigate this transition best are the ones who have thought carefully about what they're moving toward - not just what they're exiting. The ones who struggle are the ones who cross the finish line and discover they don't know what to do on a Tuesday morning.

This is not a reason to delay a sale. It's a reason to plan the transition as carefully as the deal itself. What does the next chapter look like? What are you doing with the time and freedom that the capital provides? These are worth answering before you close, not after.

The Transition Period: What to Agree To and What to Protect

Almost every business sale at this stage includes a transition period - 3 to 24 months where the seller stays involved to transfer relationships, knowledge, and customer confidence. These agreements are legitimate and often necessary for a deal to work.

What to negotiate carefully in a transition arrangement:

  • Duration - shorter is better; 6 months is common and often sufficient; push back on anything beyond 18 months
  • Compensation - your time has value; transition consulting should be paid at a reasonable rate
  • Role definition - "available for questions" is different from "still running daily operations"
  • Authority during transition - who makes decisions? If you're still making decisions but no longer have equity, that's a problem
  • Geographic and competitive restrictions - non-compete clauses typically run 2 to 5 years; understand what they restrict before you sign

Getting the Timing Right

The best time to sell a business is when the business is performing well and you're still engaged enough to present it compellingly. Not when you're burned out. Not during a revenue decline. Not when a health event forces your hand.

At 60, "when the time is right" is soon. The preparation that creates maximum value takes 18 to 24 months. If you want to close at 62 or 63, the work starts now.

The $4M seller and the $6M seller who went to market in the same year with similar businesses didn't differ in luck. One prepared. One didn't. At 60, there's still time to be the one who prepared.
